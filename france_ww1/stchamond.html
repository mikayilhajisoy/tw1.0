Saint-Chamond

The Saint-Chamond was France’s second operational tank, introduced in 1917 as an alternative to the Schneider CA1. Built by the Saint-Chamond company, it was larger and heavier, weighing around 23 tons, with a crew of nine. Its most distinctive feature was its long, overhanging hull, which housed a powerful 75 mm field gun — the same type used by the French Army in artillery units. This gave it superior firepower compared to most tanks of the time, but also created serious mobility problems. Despite its strong armament, the Saint-Chamond’s short tracks and long hull made it poor at crossing trenches and rough ground. It frequently became stuck on the battlefield, limiting its effectiveness. Nevertheless, over 400 were produced, and they saw service until the end of the war. The Saint-Chamond demonstrated both the ambition and the design flaws of early heavy tanks.

    Characteristics:
  • Type: Assault tank
  • Weight: 23 tons
  • Crew: 8
  • Armament: 75mm Mle 1897 gun; 4 x 8mm Hotchkiss machine guns
  • Speed: 12 km/h
